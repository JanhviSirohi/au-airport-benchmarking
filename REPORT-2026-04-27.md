## 1. Executive view

- **The Australian airport sector entered a broad-based capacity build-out in FY25.** Four of the five airports with extractable FY25 financials (MEL, ADL, BNE, PER) posted CAPEX/revenue above 35%; only Sydney remains capital-light at 18.7%. The cohort-wide median CAPEX/revenue is **61.5%** — 1.61× the FY24 4-airport median of 38.2% (medians recomputed against the AMR 2024-25 revised series) [ACCC AMR FY24-25 supplementary database; accessed 2026-04-27].
- **Melbourne CAPEX intensified to 86.9% of revenue** in FY25 (A$1,198 m on A$1,378 m revenue, up from A$833 m on A$1,221 m revenue in FY24 (AMR 2024-25 revised series)). The third-runway program and terminal works approved Mar 2024 are now in peak execution. CAPEX/pax reached A$33, the highest of the cohort.
- **Brisbane joined the build cycle in FY25.** BNE CAPEX nearly doubled to A$671 m (FY24: A$343 m), lifting CAPEX/revenue from 34.2% to **61.5%** and CAPEX/pax from A$15 to A$28 (FY24 figures per AMR 2024-25 revised series). BNE's parallel-runway commissioning was 2020; FY25 marks a step-change in the next investment cycle.
- **Sydney remains the capital-light outlier.** SYD FY25 CAPEX/revenue was 18.7% on A$375 m of asset additions against A$2,011 m of revenue — 0.30× the FY25 cohort median. SYD continues to carry the highest aero yield at A$29.43/pax.
- **Adelaide build-cycle continues.** ADL CAPEX trajectory FY22→FY25: A$38.7 m → A$39.5 m → A$106.8 m → A$208.7 m, a 5.4× ramp. FY25 CAPEX/revenue of 65.6% sits near the cohort median. The A$1 bn 10-year indicative program disclosed in the FY22 Integrated Review is now visible executing in the cash-flow statement [AAL FY22 Integrated Review p.8; FY23–FY25 audited financials; accessed 2026-04-27].
- **EBITDA margins held in FY25 despite the build cycle.** BNE 58.9%, ADL 60.6% (underlying), SYD 56.4%, MEL 48.1%, PER 46.9% — operating performance has recovered from FY21 trough levels and remained resilient through the FY25 capacity surge.
- **Five of ten airports in scope (CBR, HBA, DRW, CNS, plus QAL on aero/non-aero split and CAPEX) have no published per-FY audited financials.** Per-airport investment intensity at these airports is documented through master plans, ministerial press releases, and PC inquiry submissions — see §3.1.

## 2. Methodology and caveats

**Airports covered.** Ten Australian airports: the four ACCC-monitored gateways (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth) plus six capital-city or capital-adjacent airports (Adelaide, Gold Coast/QAL group, Canberra, Hobart, Darwin, Cairns). The four ACCC airports are included because they have audited regulatory accounts. The six others are included to give national coverage but Adelaide (FY22–FY25 audited financial reports) and the Gold Coast operator's parent Queensland Airports Limited (group-level FY15–FY25 highlights, 11-year P&L series) have any extractable financials in the public domain.

**Fiscal year basis.** Australian financial year ending 30 June. FY24 = year ended 30 Jun 2024; FY25 = year ended 30 Jun 2025. No calendar-year substitution.

**Currency.** AUD nominal as reported. No CPI adjustment; ABS CPI cumulative FY20–FY25 is approximately 22%, so multi-year averages crossing the pandemic should be read as nominal trends [benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md, §7].

**Sources of figures.**
- ACCC Airport Monitoring Report 2024-25 supplementary database (published 5 March 2026) for SYD/MEL/BNE/PER FY20–FY25 financial and operational time series — the primary regulated-account source for the four monitored airports.
- BITRE annual financial-year file (`web_airports_annual_fy_2024_25.xlsx`) for passengers, RPT aircraft movements, and international freight across all twenty top Australian airports.
- Adelaide Airport Limited Annual Financial Reports FY24 and FY25 (FY24 with FY23 comparatives).
- Queensland Airports Limited annual reports FY20–FY25 (group level, four airports consolidated). The QAL 2022 AR includes an 8-year P&L series FY15–FY22, extended through FY25 via the 2023, 2024 and 2025 reports — providing an 11-year QAL group P&L series (revenue, OPEX, EBITDA, interest, D&A, NPAT, dividends).
- Cairns Airport own passenger-totals PDF for CNS pax series.
- Productivity Commission Airports Inquiry submissions and ASIC company-search references for context.

**Key incomparabilities flagged below:**
- Regulated vs unregulated. Only the four ACCC-monitored airports have a public regulatory-account series; CBR, HBA, DRW, CNS have no public financials at all.
- Build-out vs steady-state phase. FY20 reflects post-investment hangover at SYD/BNE; FY24 captures MEL's third-runway and PER's terminal CAPEX pulse.
- COVID-distorted years FY20 (half affected), FY21 (full collapse), FY22 (partial recovery). Per-passenger ratios in these years are mechanically inflated by denominator collapse (e.g. SYD pax from 32.3 m FY20 to 7.85 m FY21).
- Leasehold vs freehold and single-runway vs multi-runway. Not adjusted for. SYD has 3 runways, MEL/BNE/PER 2, ADL/HBA/CBR/DRW 2, OOL/CNS 1 active.
- Hub/international vs O&D/domestic mix. SYD international-pax share FY24 = 39%; ADL = 11%; HBA/CBR ~1%.
- QAL group reporting. Gold Coast Airport standalone financials are not separately disclosed. QAL group consolidates Gold Coast + Townsville + Mount Isa + Longreach (Gold Coast was ~77% of QAL group pax FY24). Per-pax ratios in this report use QAL group financials over QAL group passengers — they cannot be cleanly compared to single-airport ratios.
- Perth ACCC pax includes FIFO charter; BITRE is RPT-only. The gap is large (FY24 ACCC 16.14 m vs BITRE 12.79 m, 26%). PER per-pax ratios in this report use the ACCC denominator to match the ACCC financial scope.
- Sydney FY24 statutory P&L is distorted by an A$9.6 bn one-off non-cash unitholder distribution recognised post the 2022 take-private. This report uses the ACCC "operating profit excluding landfill" (FY24: A$1,083.83 m) as EBITDA throughout — never the statutory line.

**Out of scope.** Shareholder distributions, parent-company allocations, concession/PPP project finance structures, and any tier-2 ASIC-paid-extract financials.

**Melbourne is in active expansion.** A$833 m of asset additions in FY24 (revised in AMR 2024-25 series) — against A$285 m at SYD, A$288 m at PER, and A$343 m at BNE [ACCC AMR FY24 supplementary database; accessed 2026-04-27]. MEL's CAPEX/revenue of 68.2% in FY24 sits at 1.79× the ACCC-4 FY24 4-airport median of 38.2% and is the only flagged upper-outlier on this metric. The 5-year average tells the same story (60.5%, well above SYD's 17.7%, BNE's 28.4%, and PER's 29.7%). The disclosed driver in the ACCC text is the third-runway approval (Mar 2024) and ongoing terminal works. MEL's CAPEX/assets ratio of 11.7% in FY24 — i.e. asset additions equivalent to 11.7% of the existing tangible non-current asset base in a single year — is consistent with a step-change in physical capacity rather than maintenance-level renewal (SYD: 1.9%, BNE: 4.2%, PER: 8.9%).

**Sydney is in capital-light steady-state.** SYD's CAPEX/revenue was 14.6% in FY24 — roughly 0.38× the ACCC-4 FY24 4-airport median of 38.2%, the lowest of the cohort, and below SYD's own 5-year average of 17.7%. Only A$285 m of CAPEX against A$1,944 m of revenue (AMR 2024-25 revised FY24). The slot-constrained Sydney runway system already in place, combined with post-take-private capital-allocation discipline, shows up clearly. SYD's CAPEX/assets ratio of 1.9% in FY24 implies maintenance-mode renewal on its A$15.01 bn asset base [ACCC AMR FY24 supplementary database; accessed 2026-04-27].

**Brisbane and Perth bracket the middle.** BNE finished its new parallel runway 01L/19R in 2020 — the FY20 CAPEX of A$480 m (54.7% of revenue) reflects the tail of that build, after which CAPEX collapsed to A$71 m FY21 and A$40 m FY22 before stepping back up to A$237–238 m in FY23–FY24. BNE FY24 CAPEX/revenue of 24.3% sits ~73% of the ACCC-4 median (33.3%) — measurable but not flagged. Perth shows the opposite pattern: CAPEX held in a tight A$108 m–A$144 m range FY20–FY23, then jumped to A$281 m in FY24 — roughly 2× the FY20–FY23 average, taking CAPEX/revenue to 42.2% (1.27× FY24 median) and CAPEX/assets to 8.9% (its highest in the series).

**The build-cycle vs steady-state pattern.** Looking at 5-year averages, the four monitored airports cluster into two pairs: high-investment phase (MEL 60.5% CAPEX/rev, PER 29.7%) and lower-investment (BNE 28.4%, SYD 17.7%). The MEL/SYD spread on FY24 CAPEX/revenue is 53.6 percentage points — 4.7× — indicating very different capital-allocation regimes operating simultaneously across the cohort. CAPEX/pax tells a similar story: MEL and PER have averaged A$22.37 and A$13.86 per passenger respectively across FY20–FY24 cumulatively, vs A$9.25 at SYD and A$13.46 at BNE [benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §1.1 footnote].

**Adelaide build-cycle (FY22–FY25 audited series).** **ADL CAPEX trajectory: A$38.7m (FY22) → A$39.5m (FY23) → A$106.8m (FY24) → A$208.7m (FY25)** — a 5.4× ramp over three years, with FY25 CAPEX/revenue of **65.6%** approaching MEL's FY24 build-cycle peak of 68.2%. Integrated Review FY22 p.8 disclosed an explicit A$1bn 10-year indicative infrastructure program; the FY24/FY25 surge is its execution phase [AAL FY22 Integrated Review p.8; AAL FY23–FY25 audited financials, cash flow statement; accessed 2026-04-27]. ADL CAPEX/pax FY24 of A$13.0 sits exactly at the cohort median; FY25 reaches ~A$24, on par with MEL.

**OOL/QAL — annual cash-flow CAPEX still not disclosed; project-level CAPEX now visible.** Across QAL Annual Reports 2022, 2023, 2024 and 2025, no annual CAPEX cash-flow line, no aero/non-aero revenue split, and no Gold Coast standalone P&L are published. However, QAL does disclose project-level capex at Gold Coast Airport: **A$500 m total precinct investment (completed FY22), A$260 m terminal expansion (Sept 2022), A$17 m ETROO (FY25), A$4 m Vertical Transport Project (FY24), and ~A$22 m loan-funded project (FY24)** [QAL 2022 AR p.4, p.7; QAL 2024 AR p.7, p.25; QAL 2025 AR p.18; accessed 2026-04-27]. Group-level annual capex remains absent and would only come from ASIC company filings. The QAL 2025 AR p.12 also notes that management is "evaluating the organisation's optimal capital structure, with a view to undertaking a refinancing mid-FY26, including the establishment of new facilities to support the group's future capital expenditure requirements" — implying additional capex investment in FY26.

### 3.1 Disclosed CAPEX programs at the unregulated airports

The four ACCC-monitored airports report annual asset additions in a regulator-defined scope; the unregulated tier-2 airports do not. They DO publish program-totals — multi-year, often part-funded, and not directly comparable to ACCC annual flows. With that caveat:

- **HBA disclosed program ≈ A$350 m+ across 2024–27** — A$130 m runway upgrade (A$60 m federal co-fund, completed Mar 2025) + A$200 m terminal doubling (completion 2027) + A$20 m carparks/forecourt + at least one further A$25 m+ MDP (Cambridge Production Kitchen, approved Nov 2025). Per-pax basis (over FY24 BITRE pax of 2.68 m): roughly A$130/pax over the 3-year program-window — heavily front-loaded versus ACCC-4 medians of A$13.5/pax/year, but the right denominator is the ~3-year build period, equating to ~A$43/pax/yr on the same FY24 base [projects.hobartairport.com.au; minister.infrastructure.gov.au; accessed 2026-04-27].

- **CBR cumulative invested ≈ A$3 bn since 1998 (~28 yrs)** per Capital Property Group corporate disclosure; ~A$2 bn cumulative as of 2018 per PC submission. Implied average ≈ A$100 m/yr over the privatised life — consistent with the airport's own PC disclosure that FY07–FY13 average annual CAPEX = 1.24× gross revenue (a regime almost twice MEL's recent 0.68× build-cycle peak). The A$480 m airport terminal (2014, 8 m pax design capacity) is the single largest line item; corporate disclosure cites a A$550 m terminal value, likely incorporating subsequent expansion [capitalpropertygroup.com.au; PC sub056 Sep 2018; accessed 2026-04-27].

- **DRW disclosed program ≈ A$300–400 m current cycle**, mostly federal Defence-funded — A$200 m runway resurfacing (Defence/Joint User, 2023–25); plus ~A$30 m hotel (2023), A$60 m diesel storage, A$11 m+ terminal transformation FY23, A$7.5 m chiller FY24, all ADG-funded; on top of the historical A$75 m terminal expansion (2015) and >A$100 m core aero infra (cumulative pre-2015). The DIA 2023 Master Plan does not publish a discrete 20-year CAPEX number but quantifies the demand-side rationale: 2 m pax 2019 → 3.7–5.5 m pax 2043; A$544 m → A$1.8 bn NT GSP contribution [defence.gov.au; darwinairport.com.au/media/278; palisadegroup.com.au; accessed 2026-04-27].

- **CNS disclosed program ≈ A$115 m+ committed (T1 + Eastern Aviation Precinct)** drawn against the shared A$155 m NAIF facility, plus a longer-horizon A$1 bn redevelopment vision (Land Use Plan approved Mar 2025). NAIF disclosure: A$1.4 bn forecast economic benefit, A$437.2 m GRP contribution, 290 construction + 1,528 operational jobs over the loan-life. Per-pax basis (FY24 BITRE pax 4.64 m): the ~A$115 m committed program ≈ A$25/pax — directionally in line with MEL's FY24 single-year reading of A$23/pax [cairnsairport.com.au/corporate/projects/; naif.gov.au; accessed 2026-04-27].

The directional message: **investment intensity at the unregulated airports — when measured over the disclosed program window rather than as a single FY — is not obviously below the ACCC-4 median.** HBA in particular is in a build-cycle phase comparable to MEL on a per-pax basis. The data still does not support a clean per-FY benchmark, but the qualitative comparison "monitored airports invest materially more than the rest" is not supported by the evidence; the difference is in disclosure scope, not investment scale.

Source: benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §2.1, §2.2, §2.3.

**Scale shows up in OPEX/movement, not OPEX/passenger.** Among the ACCC-4, BNE has the lowest OPEX/movement at A$1,855 (FY24) — well below SYD A$2,773 and PER A$2,414 — driven by Brisbane's high RPT-movement throughput on a relatively modern parallel-runway base [ACCC AMR FY24 supplementary database; accessed 2026-04-27]. OPEX/passenger is more compressed: SYD A$20, MEL A$17, BNE A$18, PER A$23 in FY24. The largest airport (SYD) and the smallest of the ACCC-4 (PER, 16.14 m ACCC pax) bracket the range, with the two mid-sized hubs (MEL, BNE) below the median A$17.5 [benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §5]. There is no clean monotone scale-effect.

**Perth is the OPEX/pax outlier among the ACCC-4.** PER's A$23/pax FY24 is 1.31× the median A$17.5 — not flagged at the 1.5× threshold but noticeably elevated. Three structural drivers visible in the data: (i) FIFO charter mix, which contributes ATM and OPEX without a proportional contribution to OPEX/pax efficiency on the wider asset base; (ii) remoteness — the operator note records terminal stock that is older relative to recent works at MEL/BNE; (iii) a CAPEX-to-OPEX ratio of 0.74 in FY24 (FY20: 0.35) suggesting Perth is partway through a renewal cycle with operating-cost overhang. PER's OPEX/movement of A$2,414 is below SYD's A$2,773 and roughly equal to MEL's A$2,464, so the OPEX/pax gap is denominator-driven (FIFO) rather than absolute-OPEX-driven.

**QAL group's apparent OPEX/pax efficiency is an artefact, not real.** QAL group OPEX/pax of A$8 in FY24 (and A$6–9 across the five years) sits at 0.46× the FY24 median A$17.5 — the only metric flagged below 0.5× cohort median. The benchmark notes flag two reasons: (i) QAL group passengers (8.34 m FY24) include the lower-margin regional airports (Townsville, Mt Isa, Longreach) that operate with much smaller cost stacks per pax, and (ii) QAL group OPEX as reported in the QAL annual report may not capture all OPEX scoped into ACCC regulatory-account convention (e.g. security recovery treatment) [benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §2.1 footnote, §6]. Read this row as group-vs-pax-denominator structural, not as a Gold Coast efficiency signal.

**ADL's FY23 and FY24 OPEX/pax of A$12 and A$13 is the second-lowest in the cohort.** This is a real number but rests on (i) AAL P&L convention (employee + services + utilities + consultants + general admin + ECL + leasing/maintenance + minor disposal — A$111.2 m FY24) which differs at the margin from the ACCC "total airport operating expenses" line, and (ii) AAL pax (8.54 m FY24, including transit/transfer/GA) running ~3.5% above BITRE RPT [raw-data-2026-04-27.md ADL row]. The directional reading — Adelaide with lower OPEX/pax than SYD/MEL/BNE/PER — is supported but the absolute level is not strictly comparable with the ACCC-4 series.

**COVID recovery trajectory.** The OPEX/revenue ratio is the cleanest cross-cycle indicator because it normalises for both denominator collapse and OPEX stickiness. ACCC-4 FY21 medians were extreme — SYD 81.0%, MEL 132.6% (revenue below OPEX), BNE 89.2%, PER 89.7% — and have normalised by FY24 to a range 42.2% (BNE) to 56.8% (PER), with the ACCC-4 median around 46%. MEL's OPEX/revenue fell from 132.6% in FY21 to 50.4% in FY24, a 82-percentage-point swing driven by revenue recovery (A$379 m → A$1,192 m) more than OPEX restraint (A$503 m → A$600 m, +19%). OPEX/pax shows the same recovery pattern: SYD A$95 in FY21 fell to A$20 in FY24; MEL A$81 to A$17; PER A$46 to A$23. None of these are real-terms — CPI growth FY21–FY24 added ~12%.

**Sydney's aero/pax premium is structural, not cyclical.** SYD aero revenue per passenger was A$29.36 in FY24 — 1.53× the FY24 median of A$19 across the five computable airports, and the highest year-on-year reading across the FY20–FY24 series at A$24–40 [ACCC AMR FY24 supplementary database; accessed 2026-04-27]. Drivers visible in the underlying data: (i) slot-constrained gateway with capped movements per hour that supports higher per-passenger landing-and-charge yield, (ii) international weighting — SYD international pax was 15.85 m FY24 (39% of total), the highest international share of any monitored airport; international landing charges are materially higher than domestic [BITRE FY 2024-25 file; accessed 2026-04-27]. The other monitored airports cluster tightly: MEL A$18, BNE A$21, PER A$19, ADL A$16. The MEL FY24 aero/pax of A$17.88 is notable given MEL's CAPEX intensity — large recent additions to the asset base have not yet flowed through to per-pax aero pricing because regulatory-period aero charges lag CAPEX recognition.

**Aeronautical share of revenue.** SYD's aero share rose from 52.7% FY20 to 62.7% FY24 — a 10-percentage-point shift toward aero — reflecting both the international-pax recovery (which lifts aero/pax) and the relative slower recovery of some non-aero categories at SYD post-take-private (non-aero/pax fell from A$23 in FY20 to A$17 in FY24). The ACCC-4 FY24 aero share range is 45.0% (PER) to 62.7% (SYD); MEL 52.7%, BNE 49.5%; ADL 48.1% [benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §3.3]. PER has the highest non-aero/pax in the cohort at A$23 (FY24), reflecting the property and ground-transport income from FIFO operations.

**ADL EBITDA margin of 75.5% is a definitional outlier, not real efficiency.** This is the single highest ratio anywhere in the file. It is driven by (a) AAL's explicit "EBITDA" P&L line which strips D&A (A$61.6 m implied) and includes property revenue at gross margin, vs (b) ACCC "operating profit excluding landfill" which is the operating-profit-before-D&A line on regulatory-account scope only [raw-data-2026-04-27.md ADL row; benchmark-tables-2026-04-27.md §4.2 footnote]. The two are conceptually similar but allocations differ; the methodology applies a ±5pp uncertainty band on cross-airport EBITDA-margin comparisons. **ADL's 75.5% should not be read as Adelaide outperforming Sydney/Brisbane (both ~57%) on real cost efficiency.** Use the EBITDA/pax measure instead — ADL A$25 FY24 vs SYD A$27, BNE A$25, PER A$18 — which is much closer to a comparable signal.

**QAL group's 67.0% EBITDA margin (FY25) is similarly a group-reporting artefact.** It includes property/development income recognition that may sit outside the ACCC scope, and runs across four airports with different cost stacks. The 11-year FY15–FY25 series shows EBITDA% has been consistently 59–70%, with COVID-distorted years FY20–FY22 at the lower end (61% / 60% / 59%) and post-recovery years FY23–FY25 at 67–69% [QAL Annual Reports 2022, 2023, 2024, 2025; accessed 2026-04-27].

**Non-aero share patterns.** Where disclosed: ADL FY24 non-aero breakdown is property A$69.0 m (47% of non-aero), parking A$37.5 m (25%), commercial trading A$32.3 m (22%), other A$8.8 m (6%) [AAL Annual Financial Report 30 Jun 2024 Note 5; accessed 2026-04-27]. The ACCC-4 non-aero/pax range FY24 is A$16 (MEL) to A$23 (PER) — a A$7 spread. PER's high non-aero/pax reflects property income from the Perth Airport Estate land bank.

**Outliers vs FY24 league-table medians (>1.5× or <0.5× median):**
- **MEL** — CAPEX/pax 1.70× median; CAPEX/rev 2.05× median (build-out phase, third-runway approval Mar 2024).
- **SYD** — Aero rev/pax 1.53× median (slot-constrained, international-weighted); CAPEX/rev 0.44× median (capital-light FY24).
- **QAL group** — OPEX/pax 0.46× median (group-vs-pax-denominator artefact, not real efficiency).

ADL's 75.5% EBITDA margin is 1.31× median — below the 1.5× flag threshold but worth treating as a definitional outlier per §5 above.

CBR, HBA, DRW, CNS are excluded from the league table — no per-FY audited financials publicly available.

## 7. Limitations and what to do next

**Data gaps (most binding first).**
- **CBR, HBA, DRW, CNS — quantified CAPEX-program disclosures available; per-FY audited P&L not.** **HBA** discloses ~A$350 m+ in named projects 2024–27 (A$130 m runway with A$60 m Cwlth co-funding [Min King release; projects.hobartairport.com.au; 2026-04-27]; A$200 m terminal doubling [hobartairport.com.au; 2026-04-27]; A$20 m carparks/forecourt). **CNS** discloses ~A$115 m of named projects (A$55 m T1 + A$60 m Eastern Aviation Precinct) drawn against an A$155 m NAIF concessional facility shared with Mackay (A$1.4 bn forecast economic benefit) [naif.gov.au; cairnsairport.com.au; 2026-04-27]. **CBR** discloses A$3 bn+ cumulative since 1998 (Capital Property Group), with A$480 m terminal (2014) and PC-submission disclosure that FY07–FY13 average annual CAPEX = 1.24× revenue [PC sub056 Sep 2018; capitalpropertygroup.com.au; 2026-04-27]. **DRW** is thinnest in disclosure: a A$200 m Defence-funded RAAF/DIA runway resurfacing program (2023–25) and ~A$75–100 m of historical terminal + core-aero spend (Palisade disclosure), with a 2023 Master Plan forecasting A$1.8 bn GSP / 7,400 jobs by 2043 [defence.gov.au; palisadegroup.com.au; DIA 2023 Master Plan; 2026-04-27]. These are program-totals or cumulative-since-privatisation figures — useful for directional sizing, not equivalent to audited annual CAPEX flows. ASIC company-search extracts are the path to per-FY P&L data; see §3.1 for program-window analysis.
- **ADL FY20 audited financials not in scope.** Adelaide audited series covers FY22–FY25 (AAL Annual Financial Reports + Integrated Review FY22). FY21 figures are derivable from FY22 Integrated Review narrative; FY20 audited would require AAL FY20 Annual Financial Report or FY21 standalone with comparatives.
- **QAL group-level only, no Gold Coast standalone — confirmed across all 4 annual reports (2022–2025).** Gold Coast Airport's standalone P&L is not disclosed in any of the four QAL annual reports retrieved. Mixing QAL group financials with Gold-Coast-only pax (BITRE 6.32 m FY24) is explicitly excluded.
- **QAL aero/non-aero split, annual CAPEX cash-flow line, and full audited statements not in QAL annual reports.** The 11-year P&L series (FY15–FY25) provides revenue, OPEX, EBITDA, interest, D&A, NPAT, and dividends declared at group level — but no segment reporting and no cash-flow CAPEX. Project-level Gold Coast capex is disclosed (A$500m precinct, A$260m terminal expansion, A$17m ETROO, A$4m Vertical Transport, ~A$22m loan-funded) but is project-total, not annual cash-flow. ASIC company filings for QAL Pty Ltd would close this gap.

**Where deeper work would help.**
- ASIC company-filing extracts (paid) for the private holdcos: Hobart International Airport Pty Ltd; Capital Airport Group Pty Ltd / Canberra Airport Pty Ltd; Northern Territory Airports Pty Ltd / Darwin International Airport Pty Ltd; Cairns Airport Pty Ltd / North Queensland Airports. Audited consolidated statements are typically lodged but require paid extract requests via ASIC Connect.
- International peer comparison. NZ (Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch — Auckland is also an NQA stakeholder), Southeast Asia hubs (Singapore Changi, Hong Kong, Kuala Lumpur), and the Gulf hubs would put AU CAPEX intensity in global context. Without this, the assessment of MEL's 68.2% CAPEX/revenue as "high" rests on within-cohort comparison only.
- Real-terms restatement. CPI cumulative FY20–FY24 was ~18%; restating multi-year averages in real terms would re-rank some metrics, particularly EBITDA/pax recovery trajectories.
- Splitting CAPEX by purpose. ACCC reports total asset additions but not the capacity-expansion vs maintenance vs commercial-development breakdown that is typically disclosed in airport master plans (MEL Master Plan 2022, BNE Master Plan 2020, etc).
- Reconciling SYD's statutory P&L to the ACCC operating-profit line so the A$9.6 bn one-off can be quantified separately.

**Arithmetic consistency.** The benchmark-tables file ran spot checks: aero + non-aero = total revenue ties for SYD/MEL/BNE/PER at FY24 within rounding; ACCC-published aero rev/pax matches derived (aero ÷ ACCC pax) to 2dp for all four airports; and SYD's ACCC operating-profit line of A$1,083.83 m is internally consistent. MEL's FY21 OPEX/rev > 100% is real (negative operating profit of A$123.56 m), not a data error. No undisclosed adjustments to source figures were made; where source figures conflicted, both are presented and the basis for the chosen value is documented.

**Refresh cadence.** The ACCC Airport Monitoring Report drops annually around late-February to mid-March (FY24 report published 17 Mar 2025); re-run after each release. BITRE monthly stats can be refreshed quarterly. AAL and QAL annual reports drop in October–November. ASIC extracts are on-demand.
