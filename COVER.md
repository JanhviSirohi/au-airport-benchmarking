## Engagement scope

Benchmarking the level of investment and expenditure (operating expenditure and capital expenditure) at Australian airports against operating size, revenue, and other normalising metrics. Focus on Australian airports.

**Airports in scope (10):**
- Tier 1 (ACCC-monitored): Sydney (SYD), Melbourne (MEL), Brisbane (BNE), Perth (PER) — full audited regulatory-account series FY20–FY25
- Tier 2 (capital-city / capital-adjacent): Adelaide (ADL) audited FY22–FY25 + FY20–FY21 derived; Queensland Airports Limited group (Gold Coast + Townsville + Mt Isa + Longreach) FY15–FY25 group-level highlights; Canberra (CBR), Hobart (HBA), Darwin (DRW), Cairns (CNS) — program-level capex disclosures only

## Headline findings (FY25)

- **The Australian airport sector entered a broad-based capacity build-out in FY25.** Cohort-wide median CAPEX/revenue jumped from 33.3% (FY24) to 61.5% (FY25). Four of five airports with extractable financials posted CAPEX/revenue above 35%; only Sydney remains capital-light.
- **Melbourne CAPEX/revenue reached 86.9%** (A$1,198m of asset additions on A$1,378m of revenue) — peak execution of the third-runway and terminal works approved March 2024.
- **Brisbane joined the build cycle in FY25** with CAPEX +95.7% YoY (A$343m → A$671m); CAPEX/revenue from 24.3% to 61.5%.
- **Sydney remains the capital-light outlier** at 18.7% CAPEX/revenue, while still carrying the highest aero yield (A$29.43/pax).
- **Adelaide is in build-cycle execution** — CAPEX rose 5.4× from A$38.7m (FY22) to A$208.7m (FY25), executing the disclosed A$1bn 10-year indicative program.
- **EBITDA margins held in FY25** despite the build cycle: BNE 58.9%, ADL 60.6% (underlying), SYD 56.4%, MEL 48.1%, PER 46.9%.

All headline numbers independently verified against primary source data — see [ARITHMETIC-SPOT-CHECK.md](ARITHMETIC-SPOT-CHECK.md).

## Methodology and key caveats

The report frames cross-airport ratios with **16 documented methodological caveats** (F1–F16 in the accuracy audit). The four highest-impact caveats are:

1. **F3 · Pax denominator scope** — PER uses ACCC pax including FIFO charter (16.14m FY24 vs BITRE RPT-only 12.79m); ADL uses BITRE; QAL uses group pax. Cross-airport per-pax ratios reflect different denominator scopes.
2. **F4 · EBITDA definition** — ACCC "operating profit excl landfill" (regulatory-account scope), AAL underlying EBITDA (excl FV gain on investment properties), and QAL group EBITDA (4-airport consolidation including property gains) are scope-distinct measures.
3. **F5 · Revenue scope** — ACCC regulated-account revenue is narrower than corporate-reported total revenue (which includes property businesses).
4. **F6 · Nominal vs real** — figures are AUD nominal; multi-year comparisons crossing FY20–FY25 conflate cyclical recovery with ~22% cumulative ABS CPI inflation.

Full 16-caveat list in [audit.html#risks](audit.html#risks).

## Limitations of analysis

- This analysis relies exclusively on publicly available primary sources accessed on **27 April 2026**. ACCC monitoring data covers SYD/MEL/BNE/PER only; private-airport data depends on what each operator chooses to publish.
- For four privately-held airports (CBR, HBA, DRW, CNS), no per-FY audited financials are publicly available; the report uses program-level capex disclosures, master plans, ministerial press releases, and Productivity Commission inquiry submissions. ASIC company filings (paid extracts) would close this gap.
- Cohort medians are sensitive to which airports are included (n=4 ACCC-monitored cohort vs n=5 with ADL); both views are presented in the league table.
- "Indicative" program totals (HBA A$350m disclosed program; ADL A$1bn 10-year indicative program; CBR A$3bn cumulative since 1998) are not equivalent to audited annual cash-flow CAPEX. They are flagged with derivation code D6 (indicative corporate disclosure) throughout.
- Figures are presented in AUD nominal. For real-terms multi-year comparisons, restatement using ABS CPI is recommended (cumulative ~22% over FY20–FY25).
- This document is not financial advice and should not be the sole basis for investment, regulatory, or strategic decisions. It is intended as a benchmarking reference; users should validate any quoted figure against the cited primary source before relying on it.
